# PP vs PET: Which Plastic Is Right for Your Product Packaging?

**PP (Polypropylene) is better for rigid industrial containers, chemical packaging, and applications requiring heat resistance or wide-mouth formats. PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate) is better for transparent bottles, carbonated beverages, and applications where clarity and gas barrier properties are critical. For most B2B industrial and FMCG packaging, PP — particularly PPCP — is the more versatile and cost-effective choice.**

---

## What Are PP and PET?

**PP (Polypropylene)** is a semi-crystalline thermoplastic with good chemical resistance, moderate transparency, and excellent heat resistance (up to ~130°C for standard grades). It is the dominant material for rigid injection-moulded containers — paint buckets, lubricant jars, ghee tubs, and chemical pails.

**PET (Polyethylene Terephthalate)** is an amorphous-to-semi-crystalline polyester known for its exceptional optical clarity, good gas barrier properties, and high stiffness-to-weight ratio. It is the standard material for carbonated beverage bottles, edible oil bottles, and personal care packaging.

---

## How Do PP and PET Compare Across Key Properties?

| Property | PP (PPCP) | PET |
|---|---|---|
| Clarity | Hazy to semi-transparent | Excellent, glass-like clarity |
| Heat resistance | High (~100–130°C) | Moderate (~70°C unfilled) |
| Chemical resistance | Excellent | Good (limited for solvents) |
| Gas barrier | Moderate | High (CO₂, O₂) |
| Wide-mouth containers | Excellent | Limited (mostly narrow-neck) |
| Recyclability | #5, widely recycled | #1, most widely recycled |
| Cost (material) | Lower | Higher |
| Weight | Lower density | Higher density |

---

## When Should You Choose PP?

PP and PPCP are the right choice when:

- **Container format is wide-mouth** — jars, buckets, tubs, and pails cannot typically be blow-moulded in PET at industrial sizes
- **Product requires heat resistance** — hot-fill applications (ghee, certain food products, industrial chemicals filled hot)
- **Chemical compatibility is critical** — acids, alkalis, and many organic compounds that would attack PET
- **Cost efficiency at scale** — PP resin is consistently cheaper than PET on a per-kg basis in India
- **Rigid stacking performance** — injection-moulded PP containers outperform PET in column stacking loads

---

## When Should You Choose PET?

PET is the right choice when:

- **Transparency is a selling point** — consumers need to see the product (edible oil, juices, spices)
- **Carbonated products** — PET's CO₂ barrier is essential for aerated beverages
- **Narrow-neck bottle format** — PET is optimised for stretch blow-moulded bottles
- **Oxygen-sensitive products** — where PET's barrier reduces oxidation during shelf life

---

## Can PP and PET Be Mixed or Substituted?

PP and PET are not interchangeable in a recycling stream — mixing them contaminates both. From a packaging design perspective, they serve different formats and should be selected based on the container geometry, fill conditions, and product requirements rather than treated as direct substitutes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q1. Is PP or PET safer for food contact?**
Both PP and PET are approved for food contact under Indian and international regulations when manufactured with food-grade additives. PP (IS 10146) is preferred for hot-fill and wide-mouth applications; PET is preferred for cold-fill beverages and edible oils in bottle format. Neither is inherently "safer" — both are widely used and well-studied for food safety.

**Q2. Which plastic is more recyclable — PP or PET?**
PET (#1) has the highest collection and recycling rates globally and in India because of its high demand in the recycled PET (rPET) market. PP (#5) is recyclable but has lower collection rates due to less developed end-market infrastructure in India. Both materials are technically recyclable; PET currently has a stronger recycling ecosystem.

**Q3. Why is PET more expensive than PP?**
PET is derived from purified terephthalic acid (PTA) and ethylene glycol — both petroleum derivatives with more processing steps than propylene. PP's simpler monomer supply chain and polymerisation process result in consistently lower resin prices. The cost gap varies with crude oil prices but PP typically costs 15–25% less per kg than PET in the Indian market.

**Q4. Can you make a 20L container in PET?**
Large-format PET containers (10L–20L) are technically possible but uncommon in India due to high tooling cost for large stretch blow moulds, thicker wall requirements that reduce weight advantage, and the superior stacking performance of injection-moulded PP at large sizes. For containers above 5L, injection-moulded PP is almost always the practical and economic choice.
